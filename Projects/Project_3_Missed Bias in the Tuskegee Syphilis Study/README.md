# Missed Bias in the Tuskegee Syphilis Study: A Case of Ethical and Scientific Failure

## Overview
This project is a critical case study analysis of the Tuskegee Syphilis Study, a U.S. Public Health Service study conducted from 1932 to 1972. It examines how multiple, compounding forms of research bias turned a scientific study into one of the most infamous ethical failures in American medical history — and what that failure teaches about the importance of identifying and correcting bias throughout the research process.

## Background
The Tuskegee study involved 600 Black men in rural Alabama — 399 with syphilis and 201 without — who were told they were receiving free health care while actually being observed, without informed consent, for the long-term effects of untreated syphilis. The study continued even after penicillin became the standard, effective treatment for syphilis in the 1940s, and only ended after a whistleblower exposed it publicly.

## Analysis
The write-up identifies and examines several forms of bias that compounded to produce this outcome:

- **Selection bias** — researchers deliberately targeted poor, rural Black men based on a flawed and racist premise that syphilis progressed differently in Black patients.
- **Information bias** — participants were misled about the true purpose of the study and denied effective treatment even once it was available.
- **Confirmation bias** — researchers sought to validate preconceived beliefs about disease progression rather than objectively test a hypothesis.

The paper connects these biases to their real-world consequences: a public health disaster, decades of justified mistrust in medical institutions among African Americans, and lasting damage to the credibility of clinical research.

## Key Takeaway
Bias in research isn't only a statistical or methodological concern — left unchecked, it's a moral one. Designing ethical, transparent, and inclusive studies, and rigorously checking for bias at every stage of research, is essential to preventing harm, not just improving data quality.
