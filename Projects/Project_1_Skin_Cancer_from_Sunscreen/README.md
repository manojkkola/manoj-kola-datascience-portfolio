# Sunscreen vs. Skin Cancer: A Risk-Benefit Analysis

## Overview
This project investigates a question that sits at the intersection of public health messaging and consumer safety: **does sunscreen genuinely protect against skin cancer, or could some of its chemical ingredients pose a cancer risk of their own?**

Sunscreen is universally recommended by dermatologists and health agencies as a first line of defense against skin cancers like melanoma, basal cell carcinoma (BCC), and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). At the same time, a growing body of research has raised questions about whether common chemical UV filters — oxybenzone, octinoxate, and homosalate among them — might act as endocrine disruptors or carry their own long-term health risks. This project weighs those two sides of the story against each other.

## Research Question
Does the risk of harm from chemical sunscreen ingredients outweigh the well-documented protective benefit sunscreen provides against skin cancer?

## Approach
The analysis was built in four stages:

1. **Proposal & Research Question** — framed the core question and scoped the investigation into sunscreen chemistry, skin cancer risk, and public sentiment.
2. **Literature Review & Analysis** — reviewed existing peer-reviewed research (e.g., Matta et al., 2019) and public health data (American Cancer Society) on both sunscreen chemical absorption and skin cancer incidence rates.
3. **Draft Findings & Peer Review** — incorporated structured peer feedback focused on strengthening scientific citations, distinguishing chemical vs. mineral sunscreens, and examining sunscreen's effect on Vitamin D synthesis.
4. **Final Report** — synthesized findings into a complete comparative risk analysis with recommendations.

A companion presentation summarizes the project for a general audience.

## Key Findings
- Skin cancer, especially melanoma, remains a well-documented and serious disease, with tens of thousands of new diagnoses annually in the U.S.
- Some chemical sunscreen filters have been detected in the bloodstream at levels above FDA safety thresholds, but current evidence does **not** establish that they cause cancer in humans — the data is suggestive, not conclusive.
- Public concern (tracked via search trend data) has risen in step with media coverage, even though the underlying science is still developing.
- On balance, sunscreen's protective benefit against well-documented skin cancers currently outweighs the more speculative and unproven risks tied to its chemical ingredients — though mineral-based alternatives (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) offer a lower-uncertainty option for concerned users.

## Ethical Considerations
- **Equity of access**: sun protection recommendations should account for socioeconomic barriers and differences in occupational or cultural sun exposure.
- **Responsible communication**: raising legitimate questions about chemical absorption must be balanced against the risk of discouraging sunscreen use altogether and inadvertently increasing skin cancer risk.
- **Informed choice over fear**: the goal of this work is to support evidence-based decision-making, not alarmism.
